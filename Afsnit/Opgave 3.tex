\section{Question 3}

\subsection{Introduce a childcare cost $\theta=0.05$ when a child is present.}

\bigskip

\paragraph{Model modification (childcare cost).}
Relative to the baseline model, we introduce a per-period childcare cost that is incurred whenever a child is present:
\[
\text{childcare cost in period } t:\quad \theta n_t,\qquad \theta=0.05,
\]
where $n_t$ is the child indicator. Economically, this can be interpreted as a policy reform that reduces childcare subsidies, thereby indirectly increasing costs for households with children.

\paragraph{Affected equations.}
The only big change is in the period budget constraint, where the baseline asset accumulation equation becomes
\[
a_{t+1} = (1+r)(a_t + (1-\tau_t)(w_t h_t + y_t) - \theta n_t - c_t),
\qquad \theta = 0.05.
\]
Thereby we see that period resources are reduced by $\theta n_t$ when $n_t>0$. In the simulation we set $y_t = 0$, isolating the effect of childcare costs. All other model equations are unchanged.

\paragraph{Simulation comparison.}
Table \ref{tab:compare_baseline_childcare} summarizes outcomes from the baseline model ($\theta=0$) and the model with childcare costs ($\theta=0.05$).

\centering
\begin{tabular}{lcc}
\hline
Metric & Baseline ($\theta=0$) & With childcare costs ($\theta=0.05$) \\
\hline
Average hours worked & 1.5863 & 1.5911 \\
Average consumption  & 2.4158 & 2.4090 \\
Average fertility (\# children) & 0.3503 & 0.3503 \\
Final wealth &  -0.9423 & -0.9368 \\

\end{tabular}

\paragraph{Interpretation (profiles and mechanisms).}
Introducing childcare costs reduces disposable resources in periods with children. Consistent with this, consumption falls slightly from 2.4158 to 2.4089. Average hours worked increase slightly from 1.5863 to 1.5911, which is consistent with households slightly compensating the higher child-related expenses by working more. Fertility is unchanged in this simulation, indicating that at $\theta=0.05$ the additional cost is not large enough to change fertility choices.

\paragraph{Income vs. substitution effects (qualitative).}
The Marshall elasticity combines substitution and income effects. Adding childcare costs lowers net resources in child periods, which strengthens the incentive to earn income. At the same time, higher effective costs of children reduce feasible consumption and can change smoothing in consumption between periods. In the simulations, the dominant observed adjustment is a small increase in hours and a small decline in consumption, consistent with the income-channel becoming slightly stronger when childcare becomes more expensive. The age-specific elasticities support this: $\varepsilon_{M,t}$ becomes slightly more negative at most ages under childcare costs, for instance rising from $-0.2207$ to $-0.2224$ at $t=0$ and from $-0.2177$ to $-0.2231$ at $t=3$. The effect is not uniform across ages — at $t=7$ the elasticity actually falls from $-0.2253$ to $-0.1523$ — indicating that the income channel does not operate identically over the life cycle. Overall, however, the change remains very modest, as reflected in the final-period elasticities of $-0.1719$ (baseline) and $-0.1825$ (childcare).
